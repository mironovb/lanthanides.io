# Impeccable Design Context
# Strategic Materials Ledger

---

## Design Context

### Users

**Primary:** The intelligent generalist — an investor, journalist, policy-curious reader, or
enthusiast who is not a rare-earth specialist but is discerning and takes information seriously.
They arrive wanting to understand *how much does holmium actually cost and why*, not to pull
API data or run procurement workflows. They can handle density but need clear hierarchy to
orient themselves. They will leave if they feel talked down to, or if the data looks untrustworthy.

**Secondary:** Supply chain analysts and materials scientists who use the element pages as
quick reference checks rather than primary research tools.

**Job to be done:** Arrive with a question about a specific element's price, supply risk, or
regulatory status — and leave with a confident, sourced answer in under two minutes.

### Brand Personality

**Rigorous. Authoritative. Accessible.**

This is not a neutral aggregator. It takes positions: it has a methodology, it marks uncertainty
visibly, it distinguishes verified from estimated. But it never becomes academic or exclusionary.
The voice is confident and precise, like a well-sourced analyst note — not a Bloomberg terminal
(which assumes expertise) and not a Wikipedia article (which hedges everything).

### Aesthetic Direction

**Target feel:** A well-designed print intelligence brief — Foreign Affairs meets a Goldman
Sachs commodity note. Spare, purposeful, text-forward. Typography does the work; decoration
does not exist.

**Explicitly NOT:**
- Startup/SaaS product: no rounded cards, no gradient fills, no hero illustration, no
  marketing copy aesthetic. Design should never look like it's trying to convert anyone.
- Generic commodity price trackers (Kitco, Trading Economics): no cluttered ad slots,
  no chaotic multi-column layouts, no color that doesn't mean something.

**Theme:** Light mode primary. Dense but not claustrophobic. Color is never decorative —
every non-neutral hue encodes risk, category, or status information.

**Typography hierarchy matters most.** An IBM Plex + Source Serif pairing is correct;
the challenge is ensuring heading scale and weight create enough contrast that a generalist
can skim and land in the right place.

### Design Principles

1. **Evidence over decoration.**
   Every visual element must carry information. If it doesn't point to data, provenance,
   or status — it shouldn't be there. No shadows for depth, no color for warmth, no
   illustration for engagement.

2. **Authority through restraint.**
   The site earns trust by showing what it knows and being honest about uncertainty
   (confidence scores, verification status). Design follows the same logic: confident
   where data is confident, visually subdued where data is sparse.

3. **Risk is legible at a glance.**
   The teal/amber/red semantic system is the most critical UI primitive on the site.
   These colors must never be used decoratively and must remain instantly decodable
   without reading labels.

4. **Density with hierarchy.**
   13px compact tables are appropriate for data — but section-level hierarchy should
   breathe enough that an intelligent non-specialist can scan a page and find what
   they came for without knowing the domain vocabulary.

5. **No decorative layer.**
   The content is genuinely interesting. The design proves this by refusing to
   oversell it. No gradients, no hero imagery, no marketing overlays. If an element
   is rare and expensive, the number in the table communicates that. Design amplifies
   the signal; it is not the signal.

### Key UI Commitments

- **Monospace for all numeric data** (IBM Plex Mono) — prices, quantities, purity values,
  atomic properties. This must never be compromised for visual softness.
- **Serif headings** (Source Serif 4) — editorial authority for section titles and element
  names. Correct as-is; resist pressure to go all-sans.
- **The periodic grid navigation** — structurally load-bearing. Any redesign should preserve
  its spatial logic; it orients users to the domain.
- **Regulatory banners (amber/red)** — must remain immediately distinct from body content.
  Risk status is the first thing a returning visitor needs to check.
- **Confidence/verification indicators** — must remain visible, not hidden in footnotes.
  Honest uncertainty is a competitive advantage.
