<div class="regulatory-banner banner-active">
  <div class="banner-title">Active Export Control</div>
  <div class="banner-body">
    <p>Germanium has been under MOFCOM export licensing since the original gallium/germanium control order, MOFCOM/GAC Announcement No.&thinsp;23/2023, effective 1&thinsp;August&thinsp;2023 [2]. On 3&thinsp;December&thinsp;2024, MOFCOM Announcement No.&thinsp;46/2024 banned all dual-use Ge exports to the United States outright [3]; that ban&rsquo;s Article&thinsp;2 was suspended by Announcement No.&thinsp;72/2025 on 9&thinsp;November&thinsp;2025 until 28&thinsp;November&thinsp;2026 [4].</p>
    <p>The general No.&thinsp;23/2023 licensing requirement remains in force regardless of the suspension. Reported US Ge imports fell sharply during 2024 and have only partially recovered.</p>

<div class="element-section info-block">
  <h2 class="section-title">Important Notes</h2>
  <div class="notes-block">
    <div class="note-item note-warning">
      <strong>US dual-use ban suspended, not lifted</strong>
      Article&thinsp;2 of MOFCOM No.&thinsp;46/2024 (the outright Ge dual-use ban targeting US end-users) is suspended until 28&thinsp;November&thinsp;2026 under No.&thinsp;72/2025 [4]. The general Ge licensing regime under No.&thinsp;23/2023 [2] remains fully active.
    </div>
    <div class="note-item">
      <strong>Defence IR optics exposure</strong>
      Ge is the dominant lens material for the 8&ndash;12&thinsp;&micro;m thermal-imaging window. NATO FLIR sensors, missile seekers, and night-vision systems are heavily Ge-dependent. US DoD has funded multiple Ge stockpile and reshoring solicitations.
    </div>
    <div class="note-item">
      <strong>Recycling matters</strong>
      Ge is one of the most recycled minor metals: ~30% of global supply comes from new and old scrap (fibre-optic preform offcuts, infrared optics). Recycling buffers price spikes but cannot fully offset primary supply disruption.
    </div>
  </div>
</div>
