<div class="element-section info-block">
  <h2 class="section-title">Applications</h2>
  <div class="applications-list">
    <div class="app-item">
      <strong>Aerospace alloys</strong> &mdash; Al&ndash;Sc master alloys (typically 2% Sc) produce high-strength, weldable aluminium components for fighter jets (Lockheed Martin F-35 bulkheads), spacecraft, and sporting equipment. Sc additions refine grain structure and raise recrystallisation temperature.
    </div>
    <div class="app-item">
      <strong>Solid oxide fuel cells</strong> &mdash; Scandia-stabilised zirconia (ScSZ) electrolytes offer higher ionic conductivity than yttria-stabilised alternatives, enabling lower operating temperatures in SOFCs.
    </div>
    <div class="app-item">
      <strong>Lighting &amp; electronics</strong> &mdash; Scandium iodide (ScI<sub>3</sub>) is used in metal-halide lamps to produce high-intensity, sun-like white light for stadium and cinema lighting.
    </div>
    <div class="app-item">
      <strong>5G &amp; RF semiconductors</strong> &mdash; Scandium-doped aluminium nitride (ScAlN) piezoelectric films are critical for 5G BAW/FBAR filters. U.S. semiconductor firms have warned that scandium stockpiles for 5G chip production may last only months under current export restrictions.
    </div>
    <div class="app-item">
      <strong>Defence</strong> &mdash; Lightweight armour plating, missile components, and naval vessel structures benefit from Al&ndash;Sc alloy&rsquo;s combination of low density and high strength. The U.S. Defense Logistics Agency is seeking to purchase up to $40&nbsp;million of Sc<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> for the National Defense Stockpile.
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

<div class="element-section info-block">
  <h2 class="section-title">Important Notes</h2>
  <div class="notes-block">
    <div class="note-item note-warning">
      <strong>Export controls in force.</strong> China&rsquo;s MOFCOM/GAC Announcement No.&nbsp;18 of 2025 (effective 4&nbsp;April&nbsp;2025) imposes export controls on all forms of scandium &mdash; metal, oxide, alloys, and compounds &mdash; under item designation 1C907. Controls apply universally to all countries and require case-by-case export licensing. These controls were <em>never suspended</em> and remain fully operative as of March 2026.
    </div>
    <div class="note-item note-caution">
      <strong>November 2026 deadline.</strong> China&rsquo;s expanded October 2025 rare earth controls (adding processing equipment, technology, and an extraterritorial &ldquo;Foreign Direct Product Rule&rdquo; covering items with &ge;0.1% Chinese-origin rare earths) are currently suspended until 10&nbsp;November&nbsp;2026. Reinstatement could shut off remaining scandium flows entirely.
    </div>
    <div class="note-item">
      <strong>Price bifurcation.</strong> Chinese bulk metal at $4,500&ndash;$5,250/kg exists in theory but requires a 4-month export licensing gauntlet with no guarantee of approval. Western retail starts at ~$9,700/kg for comparable quantities. Laboratory-grade pricing from Alfa Aesar ($261/g for 2&nbsp;g) represents a 50&times; markup over Chinese bulk rates.
    </div>
    <div class="note-item">
      <strong>Supply deficit.</strong> Global demand (~117&nbsp;t/year) far exceeds current production (~40&nbsp;t/year). U.S.-bound yttrium exports (controlled under the same announcement) fell over 90% in the eight months following implementation. At least two North American aerospace coating suppliers reportedly curtailed production.
    </div>
  </div>
