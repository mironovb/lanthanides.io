  <div class="banner-title">Active Export Control &mdash; Chinese dual-use licensing required</div>
  <div class="banner-body">
    <p>MOFCOM/GAC <strong>Announcement No.&thinsp;10 of 2025</strong> (effective 4&thinsp;February&thinsp;2025) placed metallic tellurium (HS&thinsp;2804500001), CdTe, CdZnTe, CdHgTe compounds, and related production technology under dual-use export control [1][2]. All exports require individual MOFCOM licences with a nominal 45-working-day review period that in practice stretches considerably longer [3].</p>
    <p>This control was <strong>never suspended</strong>. The November&thinsp;2025 diplomatic suspensions (Announcements No.&thinsp;70 and 72) covered only the October&thinsp;9 rare earth and superhard materials measures, not the February tellurium controls [4][5]. Pillsbury&rsquo;s 2025 summary table explicitly lists No.&thinsp;10 under &ldquo;Still Valid MOFCOM &amp; GAC Dual-Use Export Control Announcements&rdquo; [5].</p>
    <p>The 2026 Dual-Use Items and Technologies Import/Export Licensing Catalogue (effective 1&thinsp;January&thinsp;2026) formally incorporated tellurium materials under <strong>Category&thinsp;6C</strong>, reinforcing the controls [3]. Chinese customs enforcement surged 248% in 2025 (91&thinsp;&rarr;&thinsp;317 cases), with fines exceeding RMB&thinsp;1&thinsp;million for the first time [6]. No whitelist of authorised tellurium exporters has been published.</p>

<div class="element-section info-block">
  <h2 class="section-title">Important Notes</h2>
  <div class="notes-block">
    <div class="note-item note-caution">
      <strong>Export controls are permanent and fully enforced</strong>
      Unlike the October&thinsp;2025 rare earth controls (which were suspended in November), Announcement No.&thinsp;10/2025 has no sunset clause and no suspension. The 2026 dual-use catalogue further institutionalised the restrictions under Category&thinsp;6C [3]. Chinese sellers refusing to quote tellurium face criminal liability for unlicensed export, uncertain and lengthy licence approval, and a dramatically intensified enforcement environment [4][5][6]. Japan formally complained at the WTO in June&thinsp;2025 that licence reviews were taking &ldquo;a very long time&rdquo; even for clearly civilian items [25]. Buyers should plan for these controls to persist indefinitely.
    </div>
    <div class="note-item note-caution">
      <strong>Supply cannot scale independently of copper refining</strong>
      Tellurium is almost exclusively a byproduct of electrolytic copper refining, recovered from anode slimes at a ratio of roughly 1&thinsp;kg Te per 500&ndash;1,000&thinsp;t of copper ore processed [11][12]. Higher tellurium prices alone <em>cannot</em> increase supply &mdash; only increased copper refining or improved recovery rates can. This structural constraint makes tellurium uniquely vulnerable to supply-side policy interventions. The entire global market (~980&thinsp;t/yr) is roughly equivalent to the tellurium content of a single large copper smelter&rsquo;s anode slime output.
    </div>
    <div class="note-item">
      <strong>Chemsavers 6N at $243/kg: an anomalous opportunity</strong>
      The Chemsavers listing of zone-refined 6N tellurium at $243/kg for a 430&thinsp;g ingot is anomalously low &mdash; priced below the investment-grade 4N market ($243/kg) and well below the $1,770+ charged by element retailers for 5N material. This almost certainly represents pre-export-control inventory acquired before February 2025 and priced without adjustment. If still available, it represents an opportunistic buy. Once depleted, replacement stock at this purity and price point is unlikely [19].
    </div>
    <div class="note-item">
      <strong>First Solar is the single most important demand variable</strong>
      CdTe photovoltaics consume ~60% of global tellurium, and First Solar is the dominant CdTe manufacturer with ~14&thinsp;GW of nameplate capacity scaling to 17.7&thinsp;GW by 2027 [8][9]. A February&thinsp;2026 DOE-backed paper in <em>Joule</em> outlined a pathway to 100&thinsp;GW/yr of global CdTe capacity by 2030, which would fundamentally transform the tellurium market [7]. First Solar&rsquo;s secured supply chain through Rio Tinto Kennecott and 5N Plus partially insulates it from Chinese export controls, but any significant expansion beyond current capacity will require new tellurium sources.
    </div>
