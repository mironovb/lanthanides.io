<div class="element-section info-block">
  <h2 class="section-title">Applications</h2>
  <div class="applications-list">
    <div class="app-item">
      <strong>Permanent magnets</strong>
      Terbium is added to NdFeB magnets to maintain coercivity at high temperatures, critical for electric vehicle traction motors and wind turbine generators [3]. This is the dominant demand driver.
    </div>
    <div class="app-item">
      <strong>Green phosphors</strong>
      Tb<sup>3+</sup> is the primary green emitter in fluorescent lamps, LEDs, and display phosphors. Terbium-doped compounds produce sharp green emission at 545&thinsp;nm.
    </div>
    <div class="app-item">
      <strong>Magneto-optical devices</strong>
      Terbium gallium garnet (TGG) crystals are used in Faraday rotators and optical isolators for laser systems [3].
    </div>
    <div class="app-item">
      <strong>Defence &amp; sonar</strong>
      Terfenol-D (Tb<sub>0.3</sub>Dy<sub>0.7</sub>Fe<sub>2</sub>) is a giant magnetostrictive alloy used in naval sonar transducers, precision actuators, and vibration control systems [3].
    </div>
    <div class="app-item">
      <strong>Solid-state devices</strong>
      Terbium compounds serve as dopants in calcium fluoride, strontium molybdate, and other materials for semiconductor and laser applications.
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

<div class="element-section info-block">
  <h2 class="section-title">Important Notes</h2>
  <div class="notes-block">
    <div class="note-item note-warning">
      <strong>Phase 1 export controls in force</strong>
      MOFCOM Announcement No.&thinsp;18/2025 (effective 4&thinsp;April&thinsp;2025) controls all terbium forms &mdash; metals, oxides, alloys, compounds, mixtures, and permanent magnets &mdash; across 33 HS codes [2]. Exporters must apply for dual-use export licences, submit end-user certificates, and disclose application details. Military end-use is automatically denied. These controls have <em>not</em> been suspended.
    </div>
    <div class="note-item note-caution">
      <strong>November 2026 deadline</strong>
      China&rsquo;s expanded October&thinsp;2025 controls (Phase&thinsp;2) &mdash; covering five additional rare earths, a &ge;0.1% de minimis rule, and a foreign direct product rule &mdash; are suspended until November&thinsp;2026 following a Xi&ndash;Trump meeting [2]. Reinstatement could sharply escalate the existing bifurcation.
    </div>
    <div class="note-item">
      <strong>Three-tier pricing</strong>
      The terbium market has crystallised into three distinct price tiers: Chinese domestic (~$804/kg oxide), FOB China export (~$1,182/kg oxide, ~$2,000+/kg metal), and Western retail/investment ($4,000+/kg) [3]. Chinese rare earth magnet exports fell roughly 75% in April&ndash;May&thinsp;2025; even after partial recovery through gradual licence issuance, the two-tier pricing structure has become entrenched [3].
    </div>
    <div class="note-item">
      <strong>Supply deficit</strong>
      Global terbium production (~400&ndash;450&thinsp;tonnes/year) falls well short of projected demand (~700&thinsp;tonnes by 2028) [3]. CRU forecasts a structural combined Dy+Tb oxide deficit of ~2,920&thinsp;tonnes by 2035 [7]. The near-total sellout of terbium across retail element dealers and the removal of public pricing by scientific suppliers (Alfa Aesar, Sigma-Aldrich, GoodFellow) are tangible signals of how tight supply has become [3].
    </div>
